Class 1

Introduction to Operations & the Process View

The big idea: Every business is basically a machine that takes inputs (raw materials, customers, data) and transforms them into outputs (products, served customers, decisions). Operations Management is about making that machine run as smoothly and profitably as possible.

Core Concept: Matching Supply with Demand

The founding problem of operations is that supply almost never perfectly matches demand. When you make too much, you have waste (unsold clothes, empty airline seats). When you make too little, you lose sales (KFC running out of chicken, hospitals without enough PPE during COVID).

💡 Analogy: Imagine you're hosting a dinner party but don't know exactly how many guests are coming. Cook too much food? Wasted groceries. Cook too little? Hungry guests. Operations is the art of getting that balance right.

The Three Magic Numbers

You can describe any process with just three metrics:

  • Flow Rate (R): How many units come out per unit of time (e.g., 10 patients/hour)
  • Flow Time (T): How long each unit spends inside the process (e.g., 45 minutes per patient)
  • Inventory (I): How many units are currently inside the process (e.g., 7 patients in the clinic right now)

Little's Law: I = R × T

Inventory = Flow Rate × Flow Time     (I = R × T)

This is the single most important formula in the course. It always works. No assumptions needed. If you know any two of the three numbers, you can find the third.

💡 Analogy: Think of a bathtub. Water flows in (R), sits in the tub (I), and eventually drains out. If water comes in at 10 gallons/min and the tub holds 100 gallons, each gallon stays for 10 minutes on average. That's Little's Law.

Inventory Turns & Cash Conversion Cycle

  • Inventory Turns = COGS / Inventory = how many times per year you "sell through" your inventory. Higher is better (less money tied up).
  • Cash Conversion Cycle (C2C) = Days to sell inventory + Days to collect payment − Days before you pay suppliers. Apple's C2C is negative −89 days — they get paid by customers long before they pay suppliers. That's operational genius.

Benihana Case Study

Benihana restaurants achieved incredible profitability through clever process design:

  • Cooking in front of customers eliminated the need for a big kitchen (only 22% back-of-house vs. 30% typical)
  • Limited menu (steak, chicken, shrimp) reduced food waste to 30-35% of sales (vs. 38-48%)
  • Chef-as-entertainer meant labor cost of only 10-12% (vs. industry 30-35%!)
💡 Takeaway: Smart process design doesn't mean cutting quality — Benihana customers rated food "excellent" at 78%. It means redesigning how work flows to eliminate waste.
Class 2

Process Analysis: Finding the Bottleneck

The big idea: Every process has a weakest link — the slowest step that limits everything else. Find it, fix it, and the whole process speeds up.

Key Vocabulary

  • Activity: A single step in a process (e.g., "mix dough")
  • Resource: The person or machine doing an activity (e.g., the oven)
  • Buffer/Queue: Where things wait between steps (e.g., patients in a waiting room)
  • Bottleneck: The resource with the lowest capacity — it constrains the entire process

Capacity

Capacity of a Resource = (1 / Activity Time) × # of Resource Units

If it takes 10 minutes to bake a batch and you have 1 oven, your capacity is 6 batches/hour. Add a second oven? 12 batches/hour.

Flow Rate

Flow Rate = min(Available Input, Demand, Process Capacity)

Your actual output is limited by whichever is smallest: how much raw material you have, how much customers want, or what your bottleneck can handle.

Utilization

Utilization = Flow Rate / Capacity    (always 0% to 100%)

The bottleneck always has the highest utilization. If a machine runs at 95% utilization, it's almost maxed out.

Critical Path

When activities can happen in parallel (like making a salad while the steak cooks), the critical path is the longest chain of sequential activities. Only speeding up tasks on the critical path will speed up the overall process.

💡 Analogy: Imagine getting ready in the morning. Showering (15 min) → getting dressed (5 min) → eating breakfast (10 min) = 30 minutes. But if your coffee machine brews while you shower (runs in parallel), it doesn't add time. The shower → dress → eat chain is your critical path.

Managerial Levers

  • To increase throughput: Speed up the bottleneck, add resources there, or shift work away from it
  • To reduce flow time: Run activities in parallel instead of sequence, or cut activity durations on the critical path
Class 3

Variability & Congestion: Why Queues Form

The big idea: Even when you have enough capacity on average, randomness causes waiting. The more unpredictable arrivals and service times are, the longer the queues.

Why Does Waiting Happen?

Imagine a bank teller who can serve 1 customer every 4 minutes, and customers arrive every 4 minutes on average. In a perfect world, no one waits. But in reality, sometimes 3 customers arrive in 5 minutes, then nobody for 10 minutes. That clustering creates queues.

The Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV = Standard Deviation / Mean
  • CV = 0: Perfectly predictable (like a Swiss train schedule)
  • CV = 1: Moderately random (like random phone calls)
  • CV > 1: Highly unpredictable (like emergency room arrivals)

The Waiting Time Formula

Wait Time = (Service Time) × (Utilization Effect) × (Variability Effect)
Tq = p × [u / (1-u)] × [(CVa² + CVp²) / 2]

Three things drive waiting:

  1. Service time (p): Longer services = longer waits
  2. Utilization (u): As utilization approaches 100%, wait time explodes exponentially. This is the "hockey stick" effect.
  3. Variability: More randomness in arrivals (CVa) or service (CVp) = more waiting
💡 Analogy: Running a highway at 95% capacity doesn't mean slight traffic — it means gridlock. That's because small random variations (someone braking, a merge) cascade. Same with any process near 100% utilization.

Five Levers to Reduce Waiting

  1. Add capacity (more servers/machines) to reduce utilization
  2. Reduce service time variability (standardize, train, automate)
  3. Reduce arrival variability (appointments, reservations, staggered schedules)
  4. Pool resources — one shared queue feeding multiple servers beats separate queues
  5. Manage the psychology of waiting (tell people how long, keep them occupied, explain delays)

The Power of Pooling

Instead of 2 separate lines with 1 cashier each, have 1 line feeding 2 cashiers. Same total capacity, but dramatically less waiting because an idle cashier can serve anyone, not just "their" line.

Product Mix Optimization

Priority = Contribution Margin per Unit / Bottleneck Time per Unit

When capacity is limited, produce the product that generates the most profit per minute of bottleneck time first.

Workshop I: Putting It All Together

The big idea: This was a hands-on practice session applying Little's Law, process analysis, and queuing theory to real-world problems.

Key Problem Types Practiced

  • Little's Law applications: Car repair shops (estimating wait times from inventory and throughput), retail chains (how long does a product sit in inventory?), accounts receivable (processing checks faster to free up cash)
  • Process analysis: Loan review processes with branching paths, fast food restaurants (finding the bottleneck among multiple stations), pizza-making with two workers (task allocation)
  • Queuing problems: Phone call centers (computing idle time and wait times), auto lube shops (impact of reducing service time variability), pharmaceutical development (is adding a 4th R&D center worth $216M/year?)
Key insight from Workshop I: Splitting resources into dedicated lines (e.g., one hoist for routine repairs, one for major) can make things worse on average compared to a shared pool. Always consider pooling.

Memorable Results

  • A pharma company with 3 R&D centers: utilization = 80%, but drugs wait 33 months just to start development. Adding a 4th center drops utilization to 60% and waiting to 6 months — generating $216M/year more revenue.
  • At a fast food restaurant, the bottleneck was the cash register — not the sandwich assembly. Adding another assembler wouldn't help at all.
Class 5

Managing Inventory: The Newsvendor Model

The big idea: When you have to order inventory before you know how much customers will buy (and you can't reorder), how much should you order? Too much = waste; too little = missed sales. The Newsvendor model gives you the mathematically optimal answer.

When Does This Apply?

Any "order once, sell once" situation: fashion apparel, holiday merchandise, newspaper stands, seasonal products, perishable food, concert tickets.

The Two Costs

  • Overage Cost (Co): Cost of ordering one too many = Cost − Salvage Value. You bought it but couldn't sell it at full price.
  • Underage Cost (Cu): Cost of ordering one too few = Selling Price − Cost. A customer wanted to buy, but you had nothing to sell.
💡 Analogy: Imagine selling umbrellas before a forecast of "maybe rain." Each umbrella costs $5 and sells for $20. Leftover umbrellas can be sold for $2. Ordering too many costs $3 each (Co = 5 − 2). Missing a sale costs $15 each (Cu = 20 − 5). Since missing a sale hurts 5× more than overordering, you should lean toward ordering more.

The Critical Ratio

Critical Ratio = Cu / (Co + Cu)

This tells you the optimal probability of NOT stocking out. If the critical ratio is 0.80, you should order enough to satisfy demand 80% of the time.

Optimal Quantity = μ + z × σ

Where z comes from the normal distribution table corresponding to your critical ratio, μ is expected demand, and σ is the standard deviation of demand.

Performance Metrics

  • Expected Lost Sales = σ × L(z) — how many customers you'll turn away on average
  • Expected Leftover Inventory = σ × I(z) — how many unsold units you'll have on average
  • Expected Profit = Cu × Expected Sales − Co × Expected Leftover
  • Fill Rate = Expected Sales / Expected Demand — what fraction of customers you serve
  • Mismatch Cost = the profit you lose because demand is uncertain (vs. a perfect-information world)

Strategies to Reduce Mismatch Cost

  1. Better forecasting (reduce σ) — analytics, early sales data, collaboration with suppliers
  2. Faster supply chains (Quick Response) — like Zara, which produces during the selling season so it can react to actual demand
  3. Pre-ordering / crowdfunding — learn about demand before committing to production
Class 6

Supply Chain Coordination & The Beer Game

The big idea: When multiple companies in a supply chain each make ordering decisions independently, small fluctuations in customer demand get massively amplified as you move upstream. This is the Bullwhip Effect.

The Beer Distribution Game

A simulation where 4 players each manage one level of a beer supply chain: Retailer → Wholesaler → Distributor → Factory. Only the Retailer sees actual customer demand (~100 cases/week). Everyone else only sees orders from the player downstream.

  • Information delay: 2 weeks for an order to be processed
  • Shipping delay: 2 weeks for products to arrive
  • Costs: $0.50/unit/week for inventory, $1.00/unit/week for backlog
  • Objective: Minimize total supply chain cost
💡 Analogy: Imagine you're playing a game of telephone, but with ordering quantities. The customer says "I'd like 5% more beer." The retailer panics and orders 20% more. The wholesaler sees that jump and orders 40% more. The distributor orders 80% more. The factory cranks up production. Then demand goes back to normal and everyone is drowning in beer they can't sell. That's the Bullwhip Effect.

Why Does This Happen?

  • Long lead times: You have to order weeks before delivery, so you overshoot "just in case"
  • No demand visibility: Each level only sees orders from the next level, not actual end-customer demand
  • Overreaction to backlog: When you run short, you panic-order way too much
  • Batch ordering: Ordering in big batches rather than smooth flows amplifies swings

Periodic Review & Base-Stock Policy

Each period, look at your inventory + what's on the way, and order up to a target level S. The target should cover expected demand during the lead time plus a safety buffer for uncertainty.

Order Quantity = Target Level (S) − Current Inventory Position

How to Mitigate the Bullwhip Effect

  • Share demand information across all supply chain levels
  • Reduce lead times (faster shipping, faster order processing)
  • Order frequently in small amounts rather than large infrequent batches
  • Don't overreact to short-term fluctuations
  • Use supply chain contracts (buyback agreements, revenue sharing) to align incentives

GSCM Game (covered in detail in Part 2 below)

You are the Supply Chain Manager for two phone models. Key decisions: which features to add, how much demand to forecast, which suppliers to use (cheap-but-slow vs. expensive-but-fast). Scored on Gross Margin + Board Votes of Confidence.

🎮 Game Preparation Guide

🌐 What the Game Is About

The Global Supply Chain Management Simulation V2 (by Harvard Business Publishing) puts you in the role of a Supply Chain Manager responsible for producing and selling two models of mobile phones over 4 simulated years.

  • Model A: Base model phone (higher volume, lower margin)
  • Model B: High-end model phone (lower volume, higher margin)

Each year you cycle through 4 rooms (Design → Forecasting → Production → Boardroom), making decisions that determine your profitability and strategic reputation.

Sales season: May through December only (8 months). No demand outside this window. Demand is spread evenly across these months.

🏆 How You're Scored

Your composite score is based on two things:

  1. Gross Margin (Profit) — revenue minus all costs (production, setup, holding, markdowns)
  2. Votes of Confidence from 5 board members (Mia, Carla, Ankit, Matheo, Adele) — they evaluate your strategic reasoning, not just your numbers
Important: Board votes reward good strategy, not just good results. A well-reasoned plan that adapts to changing conditions will earn more votes than lucky guesses.

👔 Room 4: Boardroom

  • Carla, Ankit, Matheo, Adele — Each has a specific area of interest (design choices, financial performance, sourcing, forecasting accuracy)

After the boardroom, you return to the Design Room for the next year. Repeat for 4 years.

Strategy

🎯 Key Decisions You Must Make

🎨 1. Feature Selection

Which of the 4 options to add? Each changes demand volume AND per-unit margin. Maximize total contribution, not just demand or just margin. Check if added demand offsets reduced margin.

📊 2. Demand Forecast

How much will you sell each month? Being too optimistic leads to overproduction waste. Too pessimistic means lost sales. Use the consensus forecast as your anchor and adjust.

🏭 3. Supplier Mix

The most impactful decision. Use far suppliers for predictable base demand and close suppliers for the uncertain top. This is a dual sourcing strategy — the course concept of Quick Response in action.

📅 4. Production Timing

When to start production at each supplier? Far suppliers need 3-4 months lead time, so you order in Jan-Feb for May delivery. Close suppliers can start in May when you see actual demand.

💰 5. Capacity Allocation

How much capacity to allocate to Model A vs. Model B at each supplier? Remember: overproducing Model B is devastating (markdown = $54 vs. cost of $180). Be conservative on B.

📝 6. Strategy Statements

Write clear, data-driven justifications for each room. Board members read these and vote based on your reasoning quality, not just outcomes.

Watch Out

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ The 8 Deadly Mistakes

  1. Overproducing Model B
    The markdown on Model B is only $54 (vs. $180 cost). That's a $126 loss per unsold unit. Be conservative with B. If in doubt, produce less B and more A.
  2. Using only far suppliers
    Cheap per unit, yes — but you commit production 3-4 months before the selling season. If demand differs from forecast, you're stuck with the wrong quantity and no way to adjust.
  3. Using only close suppliers
    Maximum flexibility, but $10/unit more expensive and limited capacity (25k-40k). Your margins will be thin and you may not have enough capacity for high-demand scenarios.
  4. Ignoring setup costs
    Each supplier costs $1M-$2M just to activate. Activating all 4 costs $6M — a huge fixed cost that eats into margin. Only use suppliers you truly need.
  5. Not adapting year to year
    The board watches for improvement. If you repeat the same mistakes, you lose votes. Use Decision History to learn from each round.
  6. Ignoring board member feedback
    Each board member has specific areas they evaluate. Their advice tells you exactly what to improve. Read it carefully and address their concerns.
  7. Adding features that destroy margin
    An option that boosts demand 10% but cuts per-unit profit 30% is a net loss. Always calculate total profit impact = (new demand × new margin) vs. (old demand × old margin).
  8. Changing production mid-year
    You can modify production schedules, but the penalty is "significant." Plan well in the Production Room. Use close suppliers for adjustments rather than paying change penalties.

💡 Demand Prediction Strategy

🎯 What Drives Demand in This Game

Demand in the GSCM simulation is driven by several factors you can observe and analyze:

  1. Base demand for each model: Model A ~60k/month, Model B ~30k/month (consensus forecast without options)
  2. Feature selections: Each option you add in the Design Room shifts demand up or down by a known estimated amount. The demand impact is shown for each forecaster.
  3. Randomness: Actual demand fluctuates around the forecast with a known standard deviation (Model A: σ≈12k, Model B: σ≈15k). This randomness is unavoidable.
  4. Year-to-year shifts: Demand patterns may evolve across the 4 years. Watch actual vs. forecast each year to detect trends.
Key insight: Model B has a higher coefficient of variation (15/30 = 0.50) than Model A (12/60 = 0.20). This means Model B demand is relatively 2.5× more uncertain. Combined with its devastating markdown loss, this makes Model B the riskier product by far.

📡 Signals & Data Points to Watch

In the Design Room:

  • Forecaster agreement: If all 6 forecasters agree on demand impact, it's more reliable. If they disagree wildly, the estimate is uncertain — be cautious.
  • Standard deviation changes: Some options increase the std dev of the forecast (more uncertainty). Higher std dev = harder to predict = more risk.
  • Demand sensitivity: Note how much demand shifts per option. Some options give big demand boosts; others are marginal.

In the Forecasting Room:

  • Consensus vs. individual forecasters: The consensus is your best single estimate. But look at the range — if forecasters range from 45k to 75k, there's high uncertainty.
  • Average and Std Dev: These are explicitly shown. Use them for newsvendor calculations.

In the Production Room (as months unfold):

  • Actual demand vs. estimate: As months progress, you see real demand. Compare to your forecast. If demand is consistently above forecast, you may need to ramp up close-supplier production.
  • Inventory accumulation: If inventory is building up, demand is lower than expected. If you're selling out, demand is higher.

Year Over Year:

  • Track A/F ratios: Actual demand / Forecast demand for each year. If your forecast consistently overshoots (A/F < 1), lower future forecasts. If it undershoots (A/F > 1), raise them.
  • Board member hints: Sometimes board feedback implicitly tells you about demand patterns.

🧮 Practical Forecasting Heuristics

Heuristic 1: Use the Newsvendor Critical Ratio

Calculate the optimal production quantity using the newsvendor formula:

Model A: Cu = $70, Co = $160 - $144 = $16
Critical Ratio = 70 / (70 + 16) = 0.81 → z ≈ 0.88
QA = 60 + 0.88 × 12 = ~70.6k/month (order above the mean)
Model B: Cu = $90, Co = $180 - $54 = $126
Critical Ratio = 90 / (90 + 126) = 0.42 → z ≈ -0.20
QB = 30 + (-0.20) × 15 = ~27k/month (order BELOW the mean!)
Crucial insight: The newsvendor math says you should order above the mean for Model A (because overstock is cheap to liquidate) but below the mean for Model B (because overstock is extremely costly). This is counterintuitive but mathematically optimal.

Heuristic 2: Dual Sourcing Split

Commit the "safe" base quantity to far suppliers (cheap, long lead time). Reserve close-supplier capacity for adjustments.

  • Far supplier quantity: A conservative amount you're almost certain to sell (e.g., μ − 1σ or slightly higher)
  • Close supplier capacity: Reserve this to "top up" if demand materializes higher than expected
Example split for Model A (60k ± 12k):
Far supplier: ~50-55k/month (conservative base) → committed 3-4 months ahead
Close supplier: up to 25-40k/month reserve → deployed when you see actual demand

Heuristic 3: Bayesian Updating (Learn as You Go)

As months unfold within a year, update your beliefs:

  • If May-June demand averages 65k for Model A (vs. 60k forecast), the full-year average is likely above forecast
  • Use close suppliers to ramp up production in real-time
  • Across years, calculate your A/F ratio: if Year 1 actual was 10% above forecast, adjust Year 2 forecast up 5-10%

Heuristic 4: The "Regret Minimization" Test

Before finalizing quantities, ask yourself two questions:

  1. "If demand is 1 standard deviation above my forecast, how much profit do I lose from stockouts?" (Model A: 12k × $70 = $840k lost. Model B: 15k × $90 = $1.35M lost)
  2. "If demand is 1 standard deviation below my forecast, how much do I lose from overstock?" (Model A: 12k × $16 = $192k lost. Model B: 15k × $126 = $1.89M lost!)

For Model A, the stockout regret ($840k) is much worse than overstock regret ($192k) → lean toward ordering more.
For Model B, the overstock regret ($1.89M) is worse than stockout regret ($1.35M) → lean toward ordering less.

Heuristic 5: Watch for Demand Smoothness

The game states demand is "consistent" (evenly spread) across May-December. This means:

  • No seasonal peaks within the selling season (unlike real life)
  • Monthly demand should be roughly the same each month
  • If early months show high/low demand, it's likely the whole season will be high/low
  • This makes early-month data extremely valuable for mid-course corrections using close suppliers

Coefficient of Variation Model B has CV = 0.50 vs. Model A CV = 0.20. Higher CV means forecasting is relatively harder. The mismatch cost table from Class 5 shows that high-CV products with low critical ratios have massive mismatch costs (up to 97% of max profit). Model B fits this profile.
Overage vs. Underage Costs Always compute both. When underage >> overage (like Model A), order generously. When overage >> underage (like Model B), order conservatively. Let the math guide you, not gut feelings.

🏆 Your Master Game Plan

🚀 The Optimal Strategy in 6 Steps

  1. Step 1: Design Room — Evaluate Feature Profitability

    For each design option, calculate whether it increases or decreases your total monthly profit. The key test is:

    Profit Impact = [(Base Demand + ΔDemand) × (Base Margin + ΔMargin)] − [Base Demand × Base Margin]

    Do this for both models separately, then sum:

    Total Impact = ImpactA + ImpactB

    An option is worth adding only if Total Impact > 0. Be careful: an option that boosts demand by +5k but cuts margin by $20 may actually reduce total profit. Also check combinations — sometimes two features together are profitable even if one alone isn't, because demand effects stack.

    Watch the standard deviation: options that increase σ make demand harder to predict, which amplifies mismatch costs. In Year 1, keep it simple while you learn the game dynamics.

  2. Step 2: Forecasting Room — Set Optimal Production Targets

    Use the Newsvendor model to calculate exactly how much to produce. Start by computing the Critical Ratio for each model:

    Cu = Selling Price − Unit Cost   (cost of ordering one too few)
    Co = Unit Cost − Markdown Price   (cost of ordering one too many)
    Critical Ratio = Cu / (Cu + Co)

    The Critical Ratio tells you the optimal service level — the probability that you don't stock out. Then find the corresponding z-value from the normal distribution (use the Newsvendor tool or a z-table), and compute:

    Q* = μ + z × σ

    This gives you the optimal monthly production quantity. The key insight:

    • Model A: Cu=$70, Co=$16 → CR=0.81 → z=+0.88 → Q*=60+0.88×12 = 70.6k (order above the mean, because unsold units only lose $16 each)
    • Model B: Cu=$90, Co=$126 → CR=0.42 → z=−0.20 → Q*=30−0.20×15 = 27k (order below the mean, because unsold units lose $126 each!)

    Also compute expected profit to sanity-check your plan:

    Expected Sales = μ − σ × L(z)   (where L(z) is the loss function)
    Expected Leftover = Q* − Expected Sales
    Expected Profit/mo = Cu × E[Sales] − Co × E[Leftover]
  3. Step 3: Production Room — Dual Sourcing Split

    This is the most impactful decision. Split your optimal Q* between far suppliers (cheap, long lead time) and close suppliers (expensive, zero lead time). The idea is the Quick Response strategy from Class 5 (Zara model):

    Far Supplier Quantity = μ − kσ   (a conservative "safe" base you're almost certain to sell)
    Close Supplier Reserve = Q* − Far Supplier Quantity   (deployed only when you see actual demand)

    A good starting heuristic is to commit μ − 1σ to the far supplier (covers ~84% of demand scenarios), and use close suppliers for the rest:

    • Model A: Far supplier: ~48–55k/month (60−12=48 to 60−5=55). Close supplier reserve: up to 25k to top up if demand is strong.
    • Model B: Far supplier: ~15–22k/month only (30−15=15 to the newsvendor Q* of 27). Be very conservative — overproduction is devastating. Close supplier: small reserve, deployed cautiously.

    To evaluate which supplier combination minimizes cost, calculate Total Monthly Cost for each combo:

    Total Cost = Σ(Unitsi × UnitCosti) + Σ(SetupCosti) + Σ(HoldingCost × Unitsi × LeadTimei / 2)

    The holding cost term accounts for inventory sitting during the lead time — far suppliers with 4–6 month lead times accumulate significant holding costs, while close suppliers with 0 lead time have none.

  4. Step 4: During the Season — Bayesian Updating

    As months unfold (May through December), you see actual demand. Use this data to update your forecast in real time and decide whether to activate close-supplier capacity.

    Compute the running A/F ratio (Actual-to-Forecast ratio) after each observed month:

    A/F Ratio = Average Actual Demand (observed months) / Original Forecast

    Since demand is spread evenly across the season, early months are highly predictive of the full season:

    • A/F > 1.1: Demand is running above forecast. Activate close supplier capacity now to capture the upside. Order close-supplier units equal to the running average.
    • A/F between 0.9–1.1: On track. Maintain current plan.
    • A/F < 0.9: Demand is below forecast. Do not activate additional close-supplier capacity. You may already be overproducing from the far supplier commitment.

    The Regret Minimization test helps you decide how aggressively to react:

    Stockout regret = Remaining months × (A/F × Forecast − Committed Supply) × Cu
    Overstock regret = Excess units × Co

    For Model A (low Co), lean toward ordering more if in doubt. For Model B (high Co), lean toward restraint.

  5. Step 5: Board Room — Strategy Statements

    Board members vote on your reasoning quality, not just results. Write statements that demonstrate you understand the tradeoffs. Reference specific frameworks:

    • Design choices: “We selected options X and Y because their combined profit impact is +$Zk/month. We avoided option W because it increases σ by K units, raising mismatch cost.”
    • Forecasting: “Model A’s critical ratio of 0.81 indicates we should produce above the mean. Model B’s ratio of 0.42 means we produce below the mean to limit markdown exposure.”
    • Sourcing: “We commit base demand to FarFarAway at $160/unit and reserve PrettyClose capacity for reactive adjustments — a dual sourcing / Quick Response strategy that balances cost and flexibility.”
    • Adaptation: Reference what you learned from the prior year. “Last year’s A/F ratio of 1.12 suggests we under-forecasted. We’ve adjusted this year’s forecast up by 10%.”

    Listen carefully to each board member’s feedback — they tell you exactly what area to improve (design, forecasting, sourcing, or financial performance).

  6. Step 6: Year Over Year — Learn and Improve

    After each year, compute metrics that reveal systematic errors in your strategy:

    A/F Ratio = Total Actual Demand / Total Forecast Demand   (per year and cumulative)
    Utilization = Units Sold / Units Produced   (how much of your production actually sold)
    Forecast Bias = Cumulative A/F − 1   (positive = under-forecast, negative = over-forecast)
    • Forecast bias correction: If your cumulative A/F is 1.10 (you under-forecast by 10%), multiply next year’s forecast by 1.10 to debias.
    • Utilization below 90%: You’re overproducing — reduce far-supplier commitments or shift more to close suppliers.
    • YoY demand trend: Calculate the percentage change in actual demand between years. If demand is growing 5% per year, factor that into next year’s baseline.
    Suggested Forecast = Last Year’s Actual × (1 + YoY Trend) × A/F Correction

    The board watches for improvement over time. Repeating mistakes loses votes. Show that you analyze data, adjust, and get better each round. Avoid the Bullwhip Effect — don’t overreact to one year of high or low demand. Adjust gradually.
